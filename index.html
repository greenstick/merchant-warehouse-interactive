As a merchant, you've probably wondered what
happens from the time you accept your customer's
credit card to the time you receive payment.
Three main processes ensure successful sales.

Without authorization you won't be able to accept your
customer's credit card for payment—and that could mean lost
sales. You can obtain authorizations through a point–of–sale
terminal, virtual terminal, smartphone swiper, e–commerce
website, or over the phone.
Authorization follows these steps. Generally, this
entire process occurs within a matter of seconds:
1.
Depending on how you run the card, this information is transmitted to a
Processor (for instance, in a card-present environment, you swipe the card
and enter the dollar amount, then send this informationto the Processor).
2.
The Processor forwards your request to the card payment brand, such as
Visa® or MasterCard®.
3.
From there, the payment brand sends you your request to the card issuer
(the bank that issued the card to the customer).
4.
The issuer approves or declines the transaction and and sends this response
to the payment brand.
5.
The payment brand sends the response to the Processor.
6.
The Processor forwards the response to you (either via your point-of-sale
device, e-commerce website, over the phone, etc.), so you can complete the
transaction.
7.
The card will be approved, declined, or referred for purchase.
An approval means that the dollar amount you specified will be
reserved from the cardholder's available credit limit for future
settlement.
A decline means that the customer's card
cannot be used to complete the purchase.
A referral is a request for additional information (either from the
merchant or the cardholder) before an authorization can be issued.

The process of managing electronic payment
transactions so they can clear and be funded.
You've made the sale! Depending on your business, your customer has either left your store,
logged off your website, or hung up the phone, considering the sale complete. For you, however,
the transaction is still in process, since it must now be settled. To make this happen, the
approved card transaction must be presented to the Processor. The Processor then submits
those approved transactions to the payment brands for clearing through interchange.
You may hear these transactions referred to as 'deposit' transactions.

This is how settlement works:
1.
Typically your terminal or point of sale device automatically
triggers, or "batches" a settlement, unless further steps are
required (e.g. tip adjustment in a restaurant).
2.
The Processor forwards your settlement request to the appropriate
credit card payment bran for confirmation with the cardholder's
issuing bank.
3.

The Payment brand receives the settlement request and does two things:
A. Issues a credit to the Processor to reimburse you for the amount of the
settled transaction. The issuer then pays the Processor for the transaction.
B. Issues a debit to the issuer to charge for the settled
transaction.
4.
The issuer then posts the transation to the customer/cardholder's
account. At the end of the billing period, the issuer sends the
customer/cardholder his or her monthly statement.
5.
The cardholder receives his or her credit card statement and pays
the bill (to the card issuer).
The Processor deposits money into your bank account
to compensate you for transactions processed.
The funding process is really an extension
of settlement, and somtimes the terms
"settlement," "batching," and "funding" are
used interchangeably regarding this process.
Funding happens when the payment brands,
such as Visa® or MasterCard®, reimburse
the Processor for your customer transaction,
allowing us to send these funds to you.
Generally, you receive your funds within
one to two business days.

*Note that funding does not occur
on weekends or on certain holidays,
particularly federal banking holidays.
